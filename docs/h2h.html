The charts below look at how players are performing according to different measures. For each measure there's a plot for the Sox side-by-side with the Cubs, allowing us to compare the teams by themselves and with each other.
I've opted to do a bit more than just violin plots - I'm combining violin plots, box-and-whisker plots and swarm charts.
The swarm plot is the orange dots. All those dots, each representing a player, would normally be along a straight line - or axis - aligned with the name of their team. But overlapping dots would be hard to see. A swarm plot simply moves them left or right of the center lines so you can see each dot.
The olive lines are the box-and-whiskers plot, otherwise known as a box plot. Excluding outliers, this chart divides up all the values into quartiles with the box showing the middle two quartiles. Roughly half of the players should be within this box. The line in the middle of the box is the median. Outliers are anything that fall beyond the "whiskers," or the lines shooting out above and below the box.
Below all that is the violin or what I call a displacement plot. It's the color area where the width represents the number of players that fall along the horizontal axes. The more values at a particular point, the wider the plot is at that point - kind of how rocks in the middle of a stream will displace the water flowing around them. I'm also using the colors of the plot to quickly identify each team (black = White Sox, blue = Cubs).
These charts may be a bit hard to grasp at first, but once you get them they're very useful. Plus, I'll be turning a few of these into animated gifs at the end of the season so we can see how things changed over time.
If you want to learn more about these kinds of plots, google the names. There's ample written about them online.

Win probability added (WPA)
Black = White Sox, Blue = Cubs.


I love this plot because it so perfectly illustrates why it's important to look at how numbers are distributed instead of just summary stats like mean and median.

At least in late April, when I'm writing this, the median WPA values for each team were very close. Which makes no sense, when you consider the two teams' records. But when you look at how values for each player are distributed, you can better understand it.

The White Sox have players with very high and very low WPA values, as you can see from the position of the orange dots compared to the median line (the line in the middle of the box). The Cubs have players clustered around that median line.

The end result is that, while overall the Cubs have more players with higher WPA values, the way the Sox players' values are distributed makes the team median appear comparable.
